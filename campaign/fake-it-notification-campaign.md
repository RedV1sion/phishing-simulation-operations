# Objective

The objective of this phishing simulation campaign was to evaluate employee susceptibility to IT-themed phishing attacks impersonating internal IT and security teams.

The campaign simulated a realistic Microsoft Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) reset notification requesting immediate security verification due to suspicious login activity.

---

# Simulated Attack Scenario

Employees received an email appearing to originate from the IT Security Team notifying them of suspicious Microsoft 365 login activity.

The email instructed users to:

- Verify their identity immediately
- Reset MFA settings
- Confirm account ownership
- Prevent account suspension

The phishing simulation tested:

- User trust in IT/security notifications
- Urgency-based decision making
- Recognition of suspicious links
- Phishing reporting behavior

# Key Findings

## Positive Findings

- Increased phishing reporting compared to previous campaigns
- MFA awareness reduced some credential submission attempts
- Security-conscious users reported suspicious sender inconsistencies

---

## Security Concerns Identified

- IT-themed phishing notifications generated very high engagement
- Users trusted Microsoft branding without validating sender legitimacy
- Urgency and fear-based messaging significantly increased click rates
- Remote users demonstrated increased phishing susceptibility

---

# Remediation Actions

Users who failed the simulation were automatically enrolled in additional awareness training covering:

- IT impersonation attacks
- MFA phishing scams
- Microsoft 365 phishing threats
- Suspicious login alert scams
- URL inspection and sender validation
- Reporting suspicious emails

Repeat offenders were assigned targeted phishing simulations and additional monitoring.

# Realistic Operational Challenges

The campaign identified several operational challenges common in enterprise environments:

- Employees frequently trust urgent IT notifications
- Hybrid workforce dependence on cloud authentication systems
- Users reacting quickly to account suspension warnings
- High trust in Microsoft branding and security terminology
- Limited verification of internal IT communications

---

# Lessons Learned

The campaign demonstrated that IT impersonation phishing attacks remain highly effective due to employee trust in internal IT communications and security-related urgency.

Key lessons included:

- MFA-themed phishing attacks significantly increase engagement
- Microsoft branding increases phishing credibility
- Remote workforce users remain higher-risk targets
- Continuous phishing simulations improve reporting behavior
